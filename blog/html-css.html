What is the difference between Margin, Border, and Padding?

In CSS we are given many tools, commands, and properties to work with. They enable coders and web designers to beautify their work, making their sites coherent, easy on the eyes, and all-together more fun to look at and explore. This includes creating margins, borders, and paddings that surrounds/outlines text and/or images. Understanding the differences between the three are crucial to using each correctly and to their full potential.

Creating a border is akin to drawing a bow around a chunk of text. You can draw this box in your code editor by inserting your border class within the style block where you can give it specific properties, customizing the border colour, style and width. A border is great for isolating a specific section of your website or highlighting its contents to illustrate its importance.

Margins and paddings are like two sides of the same coin; they determine how much space is given outside and inside a border relevant to its contents. Now, you don’t have to have a border in order to add margins or paddings to your website; The way that they’re written are also very similar to how you would insert a border into your code. You can customise your margins by specifying how many pixels of space you’d like to stand between your border and the frame of the device that is viewing the website. Padding is almost exactly as your choose how many pixels of space you’d like to separate the text withing the border and the border’s outline.

When wanting to insert borders, margins, or paddings to your website, it is recommended to start with your style block where you can create a CSS selector for any paragraph you wish to give a border, padding or margin. 

    
       <style>
        .thin-red-border {
          border-color: red;
          border-width: 5px;
          border-style: solid;
        }
      
      </style>
