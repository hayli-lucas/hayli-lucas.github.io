What is the difference between Margin, Border, and Padding?

In CSS we are given many tools, commands, and properties to work with. They enable coders and web designers to beautify their work, making their sites coherent, easy on the eyes, and all-together more fun to look at and explore. This includes creating margins, borders, and paddings that surrounds/outlines text and/or images. Understanding the differences between the three are crucial to using each correctly and to their full potential.

Creating a border is akin to drawing a bow around a chunk of text. You can draw this box in your code editor by inserting your border class within the style block where you can give it specific properties, customizing the border colour, style and width. A border is great for isolating a specific section of your website or highlighting its contents to illustrate its importance.

Margins and paddings are like two sides of the same coin; they determine how much space is given outside and inside a border relevant to its contents. Now, you don’t have to have a border in order to add margins or paddings to your website; The way that they’re written are also very similar to how you would insert a border into your code. You can customise your margins by specifying how many pixels of space you’d like to stand between your border and the frame of the device that is viewing the website. Padding is almost exactly as you choose how many pixels of space you’d like to separate the text withing the border and the border’s outline.

How to add a Border, Margin, and/or Padding

When wanting to insert borders, margins, or paddings to your website, it is recommended to start with your style block where you can create a border class element that can be applied to any paragraph you wish to give a border, padding or margin. 

    
       <style>
        .thin-red-border {
          border-color: red;
          border-width: 2px;
          border-style: solid;
        }
      </style>

Here we are creating a class element named "thin-red-border" that will have red solid line that is two pixels thick. now if we were to give a chunk of text this border, we would inset this class element within our paragraph (or p) element to give us our border. 

   <p class="thin-red-border">
   insert some random text here to put the border around so show this imaginary
   audience how it's done...
   but dont forget to put something actually relevant or funny 
   so this actually has a chance of being coherant or simply readable...
   </p>

And there you go. Now, working with margins and paddings are very similar. You can simply add their properties to your style border. Remember that these properties are not restricted to just working with borders but for the sake of this example, that's exactly what we will be doing. 

    
       <style>
        .new-thin-red-border {
          border-color: red;
          border-width: 2px;
          border-style: solid;
          margin: 20px 100px 20px 20px;
          padding: 20px 100px 20px 20px;
        }
      
      </style>

Notice that we have added two new properties to our current style class to create a new one. The margin and padding works in a clock-wise rotation so the top, bottom, and left side of the border lines will have an open space of 20 pixels, and the right side has a space of 100 pixels. 

   <p class="new-thin-red-border">
   insert some random text here to put the border around so show this imaginary
   audience how it's done...
   but dont forget to put something actually relevant or funny 
   so this actually has a chance of being coherant or simply readable...
   </p>

Can you see the difference?
