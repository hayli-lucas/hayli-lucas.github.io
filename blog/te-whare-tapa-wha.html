Introducing Te Whare Tapa Whā

Typically, when you have a plan that you want to succeed, it is wise to have methods to support and strengthen yourself or else you will simply burn yourself out and set yourself up for failure. Here we will be looking at Tā Mason Durie’s Te Whare Tapa Whā model and exploring methods that should benefit us and our learning plan.

The primary focus of the Te Whare Tapa Whā model is improving one’s wellbeing. Improving our wellbeing improves your mental health and promotes a better work ethic and productivity. Starting with taha tinana, or your physical wellbeing, we can commit to a task that allows our body to grow and strengthen. It is important to take care of our body as it is the vessel that carries us through life and all of its ups and downs. Of course, taking up any sport is a great way to take care of our taha tinana but it could also be as simple as going for a walk to the dairy and working on taking a couple extra steps every day and documenting your progress. We should also commit to eating a healthier diet and maintaining a consistent sleep schedule.

Taha Hinengara (or your emotional and mental wellbeing) is the core of what we are trying to improve. Our mind is very powerful. Many leaps not taken are due to mental blocks and negative self thoughts. Improving your taha hinengara can be intimidating but we are going to focus on the baby steps that will take us slowly to a better head space. Starting with some positive daily affirmations. At the start of the day, look in the mirror and tell yourself, “I am strong, I am loved, I have the power and the capabilities, I got this.”

This is a great opportunity to bring up our next dimension of Te Whare Tapa Whā, which is our taha wairua (our spiritual wellbeing). Now, how one would work on improving their taha wairua is completely subjective and does not require you to be devoted to any religion or faith. In our case, we will start this journey by expressing gratitude through either prayer or through our positive affirmations. Try this by reminding yourself how lucky we are for even the simplest of things. We can even go as far as being grateful for the problems we have. Yes you read that right. Whenever you find yourself feeling down about how hard your workload is or how difficult a particular Sprint is, take a step back and say to yourself, “Wow, I’m so lucky that I am able to take this course and work for my achievements! I am so lucky that I am in a position where I can learn and grow even when times are rough.” It’s all about perspective.

The final dimension of Te Whare Tapa Whā is taha whānau (family wellbeing) which focuses on your sense of belonging and your sense of self. This does not have to be limited to your immediate family or even any blood relatives at all. We will on our taha whānau by committing to engage with those that we hold dear. A quick catchup once a week, or once a month can give a sense of purpose, connection with your family and strengthen your overall wellbeing.

Remember that this improvement has to be built on a solid foundation. Our land. Remember the phrase “The land has eyes and teeth.” If you take care of your home, your land, it will reward you. Keep in touch with the earth and she will provide for you. Remember to touch grass and love yourself. Take one step at a time and you will prosper.
